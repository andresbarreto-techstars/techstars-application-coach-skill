# The 11 Techstars Application Questions — Rubric

Source: Techstars Application Guide by Andres Barreto (Techstars MD). Use the order below in both coaching and the final document. For each question: the tip, a weak answer to avoid, and the strong version to aim for.

## Contents
1. What problem are you looking to solve?
2. What is your company going to make to solve this problem?
3. Why is your team a winning team?
4. Please share any traction metrics you have
5. How do (or will) you make money? What is your market size?
6. Who is your target audience?
7. Who are your competitors or might become competitors?
8. What is your competitive advantage?
9. Why is now the right timing for your startup?
10. What is your customer acquisition strategy?
11. What are your next major company milestones?

---

## Character limits (hard — the form enforces them)

Every answer is a capped text box. Write to the limit; a strong answer uses most of it without padding. **The single biggest mechanical failure is an answer that doesn't fit — coach every answer to land under its cap.**

| Question | Limit |
|---|---|
| 1. Problem | 500 |
| 2. Product / solution | 500 |
| 3. Team (why a winning team) | 1000 |
| 4. Traction metrics (free text) | 280 |
| 5. How you make money + market size | 400 |
| 6. Target audience | 400 |
| 7. Competitors | 400 |
| 8. Competitive advantage | 400 |
| 9. Why now | 400 |
| 10. Customer acquisition | 400 |
| 11. Milestones | 400 |

**Revenue and funding are NOT prose.** Revenue is entered as a month-by-month dollar table (last 6 months). Funding is entered by source (Friends & Family, Grant, Angel, Venture Capital, Other). So the 280-char traction box is for *non-revenue* signal (users, LOIs, pipeline, growth rate) — don't waste it restating revenue numbers that go in the table.

**Implication for deep answers (especially TAM):** the bottom-up TAM, the moat reasoning, etc. are the founder's *working analysis*. The pasted answer must be compressed to its cap (e.g. the whole market-size answer is ~400 chars). Build the full logic, then distill to the headline numbers and the one-line "why."

## 1. What problem are you looking to solve?
*Tell us what your target audience is struggling with and why they might be unsatisfied with current solutions.*

**Tip:** Describe the problem in detail — specific beats generic. Do **not** write the solution disguised as a problem. State only the problem.

- ❌ "People don't have an AI-powered mousetrap that automatically attracts and traps mice humanely." (This is the solution wearing a problem's clothing.)
- ✅ "Mice droppings are the leading cause for NYC restaurant owners to receive a C or lower health grade. Revenues plummet ~80% after a notice is posted, and with rising rents, 7 in 10 close within 30 days of a low-grade notice."

Push for: who is hurt, how badly, quantified, and why existing options fail them.

## 2. What is your company going to make to solve this problem?
*Describe your product and what it does or will do.*

**Tip:** Describe what it does and **how** it does it — not what it is. Cut buzzwords.

- ❌ "Mousetrap AI is an innovative, disruptive technology combining IoT, blockchain, and AI to provide the most advanced mousetrap on the market."
- ✅ "Mousetrap AI uses cameras and sensors to identify a mouse and trigger the peanut-butter bait. Once trapped, we text you and dispatch technicians to remove it from your restaurant."

Push for: the actual mechanism, in plain language.

## 3. Why is your team a winning team?
*What gives your team an unfair advantage — customer knowledge, skills, or something else?*

**Tip:** Show an unfair advantage with evidence. Lived experience and specific track record beat generic effort claims.

- ❌ "We work super hard and have 100 years of combined experience."
- ✅ "My family's restaurant was our entire household income; rats from neighboring construction forced us into bankruptcy, so I know this pain firsthand. My co-founder was head of R&D at Orkin for 10 years."

Push for: why *this* team, specifically, wins this market.

## 4. Please share any traction metrics you have
*Beta users, LOIs, active users, customer growth, etc.*

**Tip:** Show progress **over time**, not just one absolute number. A trend tells the story; a single figure doesn't.

- ❌ "We have 500 users."
- ✅ "We grew from 40 to 500 weekly active users over 3 months (≈45% MoM), with 12 signed LOIs from restaurants."

Push for: the trajectory, and the most credible signal they have (revenue > LOIs > waitlist).

## 5. How do (or will) you make money? What is your market size?
**Tip:** Answer **both** parts — how you make money *and* how much you could make at peak success. Use a bottom-up TAM, not a vague top-down number.

- ❌ "We charge $100/month and reach $10M ARR and break-even in year 3." (Ignores "how much could you make?")
- ✅ "Our annual subscription is $1,200. We project U.S. revenue of $749.4M from 749,404 restaurants and European revenue of $1.8B from 1.5M restaurants."

**Bottom-up TAM (quick version):**
1. Start with your customer (who you sell to).
2. Annual Contract Value (ACV) — what each customer pays per year.
3. Number of real potential customers that fit your target.
4. **TAM = ACV × number of potential customers.**

A small TAM is a venture dealbreaker — make the math credible and large.

> Founders struggle with this question more than any other. For the full method (segmenting buyers, sourcing the buyer count, sanity-checking) **and the option to research the buyer count and price via web search, read `references/bottom-up-tam.md`** whenever you're on this question.

## 6. Who is your target audience?
**Tip:** Define the persona who (a) has the problem, (b) knows they have it, (c) has quantified it, and (d) will act on it. This is your Ideal Customer Profile. Specific wins.

- ❌ "Companies doing any type of hiring."
- ✅ "Hiring managers at U.S. companies hiring high volumes (10+) of inside-sales roles."
- ❌ "Fitness centers."
- ✅ "YMCA fitness centers in a Metro Group with multiple locations of 2,000+ members; buyers are the ~60 COOs controlling ~1,000 facilities that could pay ~$100K per facility."

## 7. Who are your competitors or might become competitors?
**Tip:** Include existing direct competitors, existing indirect competitors (substitutes), and potential future competitors — both large companies **and** startups.

- ❌ "We have no competitors." (Reads as naïve — almost always wrong; the status quo is a competitor.)
- ✅ A short, honest map across all three categories.

## 8. What is your competitive advantage?
**Tip:** What makes you stand out from the hundreds solving the same problem? Aim for 10x, not 10%. Prompts to answer:
- What makes you 10x better?
- 80% cheaper, 4x faster?
- What counterintuitive-but-right approach are you taking?
- What's hard for a competitor to replicate?
- Finish: "We are the only company in the world that ______ for our customers."

**Most early software startups don't have a durable moat on day one — and that's normal.** Real technology moats mostly live in hardware, deeptech, and techbio. The strongest answer names the *one* defensibility being built first and the path to deeper ones — not a faked moat. Watch for the three false moats founders reach for: "proprietary data," "AI," and "first-mover advantage."

> This is where software founders bluff hardest. For the modern view (the data-moat fallacy, the four real defensibilities, the chokepoint/workflow-embedding lens, and the option to research the customer's workflow chokepoints) **read `references/moats.md`** whenever you're on this question.

## 9. Why is now the right timing for your startup?
**Tip:** What trend, market change, new law/policy, or new technology makes this the moment? Are you solving a brand-new problem, or solving an old one in a way that wasn't possible before? Point to a concrete external shift, not "the market is growing."

## 10. What is your customer acquisition strategy?
**Tip:** What can you do better, cheaper, and faster to acquire customers that a better-funded competitor would struggle to copy? Show a concrete path to your first 1,000 users (or $2.5M in revenue) and demonstrate you can acquire customers on your own.

## 11. What are your next major company milestones?
*What are your next features, or what are you learning to confirm you're on the right path?*

**Tip:** Name the next real proof points — the experiments, metrics, or releases that will tell you the business is working. Avoid vanity milestones.
