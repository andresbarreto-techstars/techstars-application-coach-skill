# Competitive Advantage — moats that hold up (+ optional web research)

This is the question where software founders bluff. They reach for "proprietary data," "AI," or "first-mover advantage" because they feel they need a moat and don't have an obvious one. Reviewers see through all three. Read this file whenever the founder is on Question 8.

## The honest starting point

**Most early-stage software startups have very few real defensibilities yet — and that's normal.** Defensibility is layered in over time, not present at seed. A genuine, durable *technology* moat mostly lives in hardware, deeptech, techbio, and foundational science — not in application software. So the goal of a strong answer is **not** to fake a moat. It's to name the *one* advantage the founder is building first and show a credible path to the deeper moats that come with scale.

A founder who writes "we're the only ones who can do X" without proof reads as naïve. A founder who writes "here's the moat we're building first, here's why it compounds, and here's what deepens it as we grow" reads as someone who actually understands defensibility.

## The data-moat fallacy (kill this reflex first)

"We have proprietary data" is the most common false moat. The argument against it is well-established: most so-called data network effects are really data *scale* effects, and scale effects are weak. Often the economics invert — the cost of adding useful data rises while the value of each new data point falls, because the easy cases get covered quickly and the long tail is messy and rarely repeats. In AI specifically, moats are shallower than founders expect; the model is often a pass-through to the underlying product and data.

So if a founder claims a data moat, pressure-test it with three questions:
1. **Does the data compound?** Does each new customer's data make the product meaningfully better for the next — or do you just have *more* of the same?
2. **Can a competitor reconstruct it** from public or adjacent sources, cheaply?
3. **Is it unique to a domain you own,** or generic? A model fine-tuned on genuinely unique domain data *can* be a moat; "lots of data" is not.

If the answers are weak, redirect them to a real defensibility below.

## The real defensibilities (the menu)

The four durable defensibilities in the digital age, plus the two that matter most early:

- **Network effects** — each new user makes the product better for existing users. The strongest moat, but usually earned at scale, not present at seed. If the product has a real network effect, this is the headline.
- **Switching costs / embedding** — the product is woven so deeply into the customer's workflow, data, and integrations that leaving is painful. **This is the most achievable real moat for early B2B software.**
- **Brand** — trust and category ownership. Slow to build; rarely credible at seed, but name it if it's the long-term play.
- **Scale economies** — cost advantages that grow with size. Mostly a later-stage moat.
- **Execution velocity** — shipping and learning faster than anyone else. Real but temporary; only durable if it feeds a compounding moat.
- **Distribution** — a proprietary or unusually cheap channel to customers. A hard-to-replicate channel has become a major source of rapid early growth (seed→Series A) and is a legitimate early advantage.

## Wrap around the workflow (the most useful lens for software)

The most current thinking on defensibility: you can't build a moat on the newest technology, because it's obsolete tomorrow. Instead, **wrap yourself around the customer's data and workflows**, and control the **chokepoints** — the narrowest, highest-value points in the customer's system where value is actually created. This rewards founders who understand the microeconomics of their customer's industry deeply enough to find the structural bottleneck and own it. Customer intimacy, workflow integration, and earned trust are the moats that compound even as the underlying tech keeps shifting. For a software founder with no obvious moat, this is usually the most honest and persuasive answer: *deep workflow embedding at a specific chokepoint.*

## How to coach or critique this answer

1. If the founder claims "proprietary data," "AI," or "first-mover," run the data-moat pressure-test and redirect unless it truly holds.
2. Identify which real defensibility (from the menu) actually fits their business. For most early software startups it's **switching costs / workflow embedding** and/or **distribution**, with network effects or brand as the *future* moat.
3. Frame the answer as **"the moat we're building first → why it compounds → what deepens it at scale."** Honesty about sequencing beats a fake day-one moat.
4. Make it concrete with the guide's prompts: What makes you 10x better (not 10%)? 80% cheaper or 4x faster? What counterintuitive approach is right? What is genuinely hard to replicate? Finish: "We are the only company in the world that ______ for our customers."

## Optional: research the customer's chokepoints

Finding the defensible chokepoint requires understanding the customer's workflow and industry economics — which is exactly what a founder can research. When the founder is stuck on this question, offer:

> "Finding your moat usually means finding the chokepoint in your customer's workflow — the narrow, high-value step where you can embed so deeply that leaving hurts. Want me to research your space for where those bottlenecks and switching costs tend to be?"

If yes, run several targeted searches: how the target customer's core workflow operates today, where the expensive or high-friction steps are, what tools they already use and how entrenched those are (to gauge switching costs), and how incumbents in the space defend their position. Use what you find to help the founder name a specific, defensible chokepoint — then write the answer around it. For a broad or unfamiliar industry, mention they can use Claude's Research feature for a deeper pass. Never manufacture a moat the evidence doesn't support; if the honest conclusion is "the moat is early and being built," say that clearly and frame the sequencing.
